IoT terms and definitions to know

Entity
Anyone, or anything that uses IoT. Includes businesses, governments, and consumers.
Physical layer
The IoT device hardware, including sensors and components that transmit the data
Network layer
Transmitting the data collected by the physical layer to different devices
Application layer
This includes the protocols and interfaces that devices use to identify and communicate with each other
Remotes
Enables entities that utilize IoT devices to connect with and control them using a dashboard, such as a mobile application. They include smartphones, tablets, PCs, smartwatches, connected TVs, and nontraditional remotes
Dashboard
Display information about the IoT device to users and enables them to control their IoT device. It is generally housed on a remote
Analytics
Software system that analyze the data generated by IoT devices
Data storage
Where data from IoT devices is stored
Networks
The internet communication layer that enables the entity to communicate with their device, and sometimes enables devices to communicate with each other

IoT and Security

All these connected devices is creating a big security problem. IoT is still in its infancy stage, and industry standards for how these devices connect to the internet and secure the data that they upload hasn't been agreed upon yet. This is making it difficult for people to protect their devices, and their data. One data point might not reveal a whole lot. Having a light turn on at 3am might not mean much, but add in a few more data points from a wearable device that tracks not only your heart rate, but also your exercise route and sleeping habits, then a company, or some nefarious person, could develop a pretty good picture on the person who uploaded that data.

With all these devices connected to the internet, it becomes even more important to make sure passwords and security protocols are being followed. The best advice when setting up a new device that can connect to the internet is to change any manufacturer username and passwords that are supplied to set up the device to Wi-fi. Most people do not change these "admin" settings, and it is these settings that hackers take advantage of to gain control of these devices.

One dramatic case happened in 2016, when 3 college students managed to commandeer unsecured IoT devices throughout the world, and used them to launch a targeted distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attack that slowed or stopped the internet in France and the eastern United States. These college students took advantage of people not changing the default factory security settings, managed to infect roughly 600,000 devices, and then used those devices to launch their attacks. Luckily, the students' goal wasn't to cripple the Internet, or anything else too criminal. Their main focus was a cut-throat competition to gain subscribers for their Minecraft server.

HP reseachers had tested 10 IoT home security systems for how secure those systems were. Of those 10 devices they tested, all 10 had security problems. One researcher had stated that with one of these systems the homeowner may not be the only one monitoring the home. The HP researchers found:

  • 100% allowed the use of weak passwords
  • 100% lacked an account lockout mechanism that would prevent automation attacks
  • 100% were vulnerable to account harvesting, allowing attackers to guess login credentials and gain access
  • 4 of 7 systems that had cameras, gave the owner the ability to grant video access to additional users, further excerbating account harvesting issues
  • Two of the systems allowed video to be streamed locally without authentication
  • A single system offered two-factor authentication

In 2014, a website, with a Russian IP address, exposed the security issue of failing to changing the default username and password that comes with IoT home security cameras. The website showed more than 72,000 cameras, live streaming, from 256 countries around the world. The top 10 counties the website listed in 2014:

  1. 11,046 in the U.S.
  2. 6,536 in South Korea
  3. 4,770 in China
  4. 3,359 in Mexico
  5. 3,285 in France
  6. 2,870 in Italy
  7. 2,422 in the U.K.
  8. 2,268 in the Netherlands
  9. 2,220 in Colombia
  10. 1,970 in India

The number of IoT devices is exploding. It is expected 20 to 30 billion IoT devices will be connected to the internet by 2020. This has prompted lawmakers to find ways to ensure that the publics' privacy and data are protected. As the number of IoT devices continues to grow exponentially, the need for security standards for IoT devices will grow even faster.
